#### What Ben Brantley Stepping Down Means to the Future of Theatre

In somewhat of a surprise, the most influential critic in all of American Theatre¬†[decided to step down from his post](https://www.nytco.com/press/ben-brantley-take-a-bow/)¬†this past week after holding the position of Chief Critic of the New York Times theatre section for the last 24 years.

The reactions to Ben Brantley‚Äôs departure have been mixed, to say the least. I hope that Brantley‚Äôs departure will have released some of that power, but I don‚Äôt think that will be completely true until the entire theatre industry is less top-down, more completely democratized.

Still, this is a no-brainer chance for the New York Times to hire a non-white-male critic who could have a significant role in shaping the future of New York Theater. They already have some good writers; I‚Äôve enjoyed the work of somewhat recent addition,¬†[Maya Phillips](https://www.nytimes.com/by/maya-phillips), who writes about theatre, movies, and more. We‚Äôll see what direction they go, but as long as this position holds the extreme power that it does, I hope they hire someone who can bring more people into the theatre, and whose artistic taste and sensibilities also match with the moment.¬†

#### The tension between live and filmed theatre

Creating theatre-to-be-filmed isn‚Äôt as simple as just ‚Äúfilming theatre‚Äù, and what works well on stage may not work well on screen-as-stage. One of the biggest impediments with streaming theatre, though, is the labor agreements surrounding it in the US. That‚Äôs primarily because of a lack of agreement between producers and Actors‚Äô Equity on how actors can work in streaming theatre. #### How does theatre need to change?

The New York Times¬†[asked some of their critics about ‚Äúhow to birth a new American theater‚Äù](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/09/11/theater/how-to-change-theater.html). > The Negro is already in the theater and has been there for a long time; but his presence there is not yet thoroughly normal. His audience is mainly a white audience and the Negro actor has, for a long time, been asked to entertain this more or less alien group.

One point I‚Äôd like to add: if we want to open up the canon‚Äîwhich we definitely should!‚Äîthen we also need a system that supports more strong young, diverse, creative directors, and enables them to tackle old plays. New York theatre simultaneously empowers both playwrights and a particular type of director: primarily the male, Euro-chic inspired one. I‚Äôve¬†[written before](https://www.guscuddy.com/europeanvamerican)¬†about how there has been a proliferation of amazing playwrights in America recently (arguably more so than anywhere else in the world), but how New York still can‚Äôt trust anyone but white males to be ‚Äúauteur‚Äù directors. I‚Äôd like to see a future where a diverse set of directors are enabled to truly dive into older plays from playwrights that have been written out of the canon‚Äîand given the European freedom to not have total reverence to the text.
